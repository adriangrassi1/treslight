CHAPTER I — SPAIN · SIERRA NEVADA
Mirador
Fin del Mundo
A dirt road, a vanishing point, and the strange feeling of being exactly where you're supposed to be.
February 27, 2026 · Sierra Nevada, Spain · 6 min read
THE ROAD IN
No GPS signal.
No plan.

I found the Mirador Fin del Mundo by accident. I was driving a dirt road I wasn't supposed to be on — the California bouncing on red earth, dust rising behind me — when the valley opened up and I stopped breathing for a second.

The Sierra Nevada was ahead of me, white and heavy with snow. Below, the landscape looked like nothing I'd seen before — red clay canyons carved by centuries of water, a green field running along the edge like a wound healing slowly.

"The end of the world. That's what they call this place. Standing there, it made perfect sense."

There were two other vans parked when I arrived. Nobody spoke. We just looked.

THE PLACE

This part of Spain doesn't look like Spain. It looks like something between Utah and the moon. The soil is deep red, the rocks layered like sediment left behind by a sea that no longer exists. Villages are carved directly into the cliffs — white walls emerging from the terracotta stone, a surreal geometry that took centuries to build.

I walked the dirt road down toward the lake. The water was an impossible shade of turquoise — too vivid, too still, almost artificial. Around it, the eroded gullies and ridges of the badlands stretched as far as I could see.

I had no internet. No signal. I ate lunch sitting on a rock looking at the water. One of the best meals of the trip.

THE VAN
Home, for one more night

I slept in the California that night, parked on the ridge above the canyon. No light pollution. The kind of sky you forget exists.

There's something about sleeping in a van in a place like this that resets something inside you. The world gets quiet in a way it never does at home. You stop checking your phone — not because you decided to, but because you simply forget it exists.

"The California has 105,000 km on it. It still starts every morning."

I drove out the next morning on the same dirt road, dust rising behind me. I didn't know where I was going. I knew I was in no hurry to get there.
